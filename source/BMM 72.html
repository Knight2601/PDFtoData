Large slaughterhouses step
up feedlot activities
The five largest slaughterhouses
in Brazil have about 330,000
head of cattle in their own
feedlots, reveals an exclusive
survey carried out by AgraFNP.
This is approximately 15% of all
the cattle confined in Brazil and
is considerably higher than the
60,000 head estimated two years
ago.
Analysts say that with this
significant increase in the
number of cattle confined, the
companies have reduced
supply shocks during the year.
In Brazil just 5% of beef cattle
fattening is intensive. However,
in the second half of the year,
when pastureland is run down,
this percentage can rise to over
10% of the supply of steers.
This year, the volume of
animals available was already
lower before the official
beginning of winter, on June
21
st
. This is because total cattle
numbers have fallen after an
intense period of slaughtering
cows.
AgraFNP estimates Brazil's
cattle herd at 159.3 million head
this year, which would mean a
3.5% reduction on 2006. Three
years ago the number was 173
million head. The number of
steers slaughtered this year is
forecast at 44 million head,
compared with almost 47
million in 2006 – a considerable
reduction.
As a result of this reduced
supply, steer prices have risen
almost 10% since the beginning
of the year, getting close to
R$60.00 per arroba (about
US$0.95/pound) in the state of
São Paulo. The market is now
asking how much higher steers
prices can go based on the
current scenario; that is, without
any surprise factors entering
the frame.
The proportion of cattle
available in slaughterhouses'
own feedlots will certainly be an
important factor as regards
prices in the off-season. The
volume is not yet sufficient to
calm the industry as regards
cattle supply. However, it is
large enough to scare farmers.
The expansion of feedlots
owned by slaughterhouses
follows a trend in Brazilian
agribusiness towards the
verticalization of production.
Today the best example of this
movement is to be seen in the
orange juice industry, which
has invested heavily in buying
its own groves.
However, in the meat industry
analysts are questioning how
much is to be gained from this
strategy, as having their own
feedlots does not eliminate the
cost of buying steers and
calves to be fattened – it simply
transfers the problem to an
earlier link in the productive
chain.

BEEF Market Overview
Last week the volume of business on the live cattle
spot market was relatively small. A reduction in
supply has hampered the purchase of animals, and
those buyers unwilling to pay higher prices have
reduced their live cattle commitments.
In the region of Barretos (SP), animals were traded at
R$59 per arroba (15kg). In the border region of Rio
Grande do Sul (RS) supply has fallen and prices
have risen to R$61.50/@ for steers and R$57/@ for
cows. In the region of Goiânia (GO) prices are firmer
at R$56/@ and plant entry terms in the region are at
four days on average.
In the region of Paragominas (PA), there has been a
similar drop in supply to that seen elsewhere in
Brazil. At the same time, the region has been
shipping cattle to Venezuela, making it even more
difficult for local slaughterhouses to secure supplies.
Over the next few days, prices should continue to
rise. Buyers are under growing pressure to pay more
for cattle, as they find it increasingly difficult to cover
their slaughtering requirements.
On the wholesale beef market, meat sales were very
slow last week, but again the lack of supply is
buoying prices. This situation is expected to continue
with prices remaining high despite sluggish sales.

New requirements enter force for
exports to Russia
Brazilian slaughterhouses exporting beef to Russia
have until June 20th to adopt new control and
primary packaging measures.
From this date all beef sold to Russia must have a
label carrying the production date, as well as a
Federal Inspection Service (SIF) seal and the
destination.
The security code on the primary packaging goes
from the slaughter of the animal to shipping of the
final product.
"Without this measure there will be no certification,
blocking exports to Russia," explained the secretary
of Livestock Farming Defence at the Ministry of
Agriculture, Inácio Kroetz.
Kroetz says that from July 1st other security
measures will be added. The new document will be
printed on special paper carrying security details to
prevent fraud.
Kroetz says the measures are nothing new for the
Brazilian government, as they have been adopted in
trade between Brazil, the European Union and the
USA.

Brazil takes stock after trade talks
collapse
Brazilian farm exports will continue to rise despite last
week's collapse of negotiations between the four
leading players in the WTO Doha Round
negotiations, Brazil's foreign minister and top trade
official said.
"You can be sure, Brazil will go on exporting more
and more," he said, noting that previous breakdowns
in negotiations have failed to halt the rise of Brazilian
exports. "We are not going to be doom-mongers.
Trade is going to carry on," he added.
The minister vowed to continue fighting to open world
agricultural markets, but admitted that the breakdown
of talks in Potsdam spelt the end of the so-called 'G4'
group, made up of Brazil, India, the EU and the US.
'The G4 as such is dead,' Amorim said bluntly.
Still, it is 'difficult but not impossible' for all 150
members of the WTO to arrive at a comprehensive
global trade deal, he added.
Amorim said Brazil will also look at other avenues for
boosting trade outside of WTO talks. "We want to
expand our agreement with India and strengthen
relations in Mercosur and Latin America," he said.
Amorim and Indian Commerce Minister Kamal Nath
walked out of the G4 talks yesterday, blaming the US
and EU for failing to meet their demands to cut
agriculture subsidies. Meanwhile, the EU and US
representatives accused the two emerging nations of
refusing to budge on reducing import tariffs on
industrial goods.
Perdigão buys cattle
slaughterhouse in Mato Grosso
Perdigão, the second largest chicken processor in
Brazil, has announced the R$100 million purchase of
a cattle slaughterhouse in the State of Mato Grosso,
with a capacity to slaughter 500 head/day. The
forecast is to boost it to 2,000 head/day.
The unit exports to Russia and the UAE, Angola,
Kuwait, Morocco, the Caribbean and Georgia,
amongst others, and will be able to trade its products
in the European Union.
Perdigão has operated in the cattle segment since
September 2005, slaughtering 550 to 600 cattle a
day.
The company aims to slaughter 6,000 animals a day
by 2011 and for the beef market to account for 12%
of its turnover, forecast to be R$12 billion.
Marfrig plans expansion on prime
meat market
To boost the supply of premium meat, Marfrig, which
is amongst the top five slaughterhouses in Brazil, has
decided to incorporate the European breed Angus
into its production line.
This month the company begins slaughtering 1,000
animals a month. The target is that Angus cattle will
account for 10% of the group's slaughter in four or
five years, says Roberto Barcellos, Special Projects
manager at the company.
Marfrig today imports Angus cattle to Brazil from its
units in Argentina and Uruguay, to supply Brazilian
restaurants. The aim with the new project is to supply
these clients domestically.
Angus meat is marbled, making it more succulent.
Inspectors' strike could make
Brazilian exports dearer
A planned strike by Brazilian Federal livestock
inspectors will have an effect on Brazilian export
prices, hampering competitiveness on the
international market.
So says the vice-president of the São Paulo State
Maritime Agency Union (Sindamar), José Roque.
"The cost of a ship docked in port is US$25,000 to
US$45,000 a day and strikes make the ships spend
more time docked, because the holds have to be
inspected before setting sail," explained Roque. This
includes fruit and meats that are transported in
refrigerated containers.
Brazilian beef to benefit from
biofuels boom
Growing world demand for biofuels is generally seen
as a threat to livestock producers, as competition for
grains pushes up the price of feed materials. But
Brazil's beef industry could actually benefit from the
expansion of ethanol and biodiesel production in the
US and Europe, according to a leading figure on
Brazil's commodity markets.
Ivan Wedekin, agribusiness director for the Brazilian
Commodities & Futures Exchange, or BM&F, said
higher maize prices because of ethanol was pushing
up the price of animal feed in the US. He said
demand for biofuels in general was also raising feed
prices in Europe - a major market for Brazilian beef
exports.

with the expansion of ethanol," Wedekin told local

"In the US, 80% of the cattle are fed a mix of maize-

Brazil creates standard for early-
maturing calf
The Brazilian Association of Early-Maturing Calves
(ABNP) has officially launched the first norm defining
this kind of calf in Brazil.
The entity plans to promote these standards and go
forward with international certification to add value to
the premium meat produced in Brazil.
On the domestic market alone the premiums paid by
slaughterhouses for meat from these calves is
expected to rise by 5% to at least 10% with
certification, according to the president of the ABNP,
Constantino Ajimastro Junior.
The target market is farmers who use high levels of
technology and which represent less than 1% of the
2.7 million breeders in Brazil.
"They are state-of-the-art producers, who are familiar
with international standards," explained Ajimastro
Junior.
Brazilian livestock farming labour
costs up
Labour costs on Brazilian livestock farms have risen
90% since 2003 in the States surveyed by the
Brazilian Agriculture and Livestock Confederation
(CNA). In the same period, the price of steers has
fallen 7.2%.
"Despite rising costs that have cut profitability,
Brazilian livestock farming continues to break export
records," said Antenor Nogueira, from the CNA.
However, according to the CNA, gains made by
exports are still not getting through to producers,
compromising their investment capacity.
"If this continues, it could harm productivity in the
sector in the medium and long term," said Nogueira.
Despite the rise, Brazil still has much lower labour
costs than most of its competitors on world beef
markets. Austria has the highest labour cost in the
world, varying from US$139.00 to US$ 276.00 per
100 kg of carcass sold, whilst the European average
is US$ 80.00/100 kg sold.
Slaughterhouses in Northern Brazil
face tax dodge probe
The Brazilian tax authorities and the Federal Police
have announced an operation against
slaughterhouses in the North of Brazil, in the States
of Maranhão, Pará and Tocantins.
The companies are suspected of having dodged
federal taxes worth at least R$2 billion (about US$1
billion) in the last five years.
Investigations began a year ago. It was discovered
that slaughterhouses used front companies to dodge
taxes, welfare and labour contributions.
In Brazil the beef industry is going through a period of
transformation, changing from a disjointed and
disorganized sector into a concentrated chain being
rapidly turned into a professional organization.
Large groups in the sector have gone public and
expanded internationally, consolidating their high-
profile positions on the international market.
At the same time, some small and medium sized
companies focused on the domestic market have
resorted to illegal means, as they struggle to avoid
bankruptcy.
Slaughterhouses want agreement
on cartel accusation
After a process that has already lasted two years,
Brazilian livestock farmers and slaughterhouses will
arrive at an agreement about the accusation of
forming a cartel, which is being looked into by the
Economic Defence Administrative Council (CADE).
With the law that authorizes CADE to make accords
between the parties, some slaughterhouses have
contacted the body that represents the livestock
farmers, the CNA, to find out if there is any interest in
the sector in interrupting the case.
"This interest exists, but it will only firm up if there is
an agreement between all the slaughterhouses in
Brazil and not just those involved in the case," said
Antenor Nogueira, president of the livestock centre at
the CNA.

Argentine officials accused of FMD
failings
Animal health officials in Argentina face new charges
over alleged failings in the way they responded to a
foot and mouth disease outbreak (FMD) in February
2006.
A case is being brought by a rancher - José Romero
Brisco – who had to destroy more than 4,000 animals
after FMD was detected on his property in the
northern province of Corrientes. Initial investigations
carried out by the Food and Animal Health Authority
(SENASA) suggested Romero Brisco was guilty of
bringing infected animals into the country.
But these charges were subsequently dropped with
the rancher granted more than 2 million pesos
(US$700,000) in compensation.
Now the tables have been turned with Romero Brisco
accusing four SENASA officials of a series of 'abuses
and arbitrary actions'. The rancher claims that cases
of FMD on a neighbouring property were not properly
investigated, while inadequate steps were taken to
control an outbreak in nearby Rincon Navarro.
Romero Brisco said the case was being not being
taken against SENASA as an institution, but against
individual officials who 'failed to fulfill their duties to
prevent the spread of the epidemic'.
"We are seeking a definitive answer about where the
outbreak originated and if information was hidden,"
said the rancher, adding that at the time of the
outbreak, his own animals had all been given the
necessary vaccinations against FMD.
Venezuela: Chavez threatens to
confiscate farms
To contain the growing lack of certain foodstuffs on
supermarket shelves in Venezuela, the country's
president, Hugo Chavez, has threatened to take
control of dairy and livestock farms that refuse to sell
at government-regulated prices.
Chavez has said he has asked for help from the
Army to inspect and, if necessary, confiscate the
farms. In a speech on state TV he told farmers that
the revolution is coming and they need to take a new
attitude towards production.
According to businesspeople, the shortage of food is
down to strict government price controls. The
government blames speculation by producers.
Chavez, who has not discarded the possibility of
importing these items, says Venezuela is going
through a transition period from capitalism to
socialism, which is why it does not yet produce
enough.
In answer, the president of the largest business
federation in Venezuela has said confiscation will not
solve the shortages. For him, producers need
government incentives.

70%
The European Union tabled an offer at last week's ill-
fated G-4 meeting in Potsdam to cut import tariffs on
its most heavily-protected agricultural products by up
to 70%, according to unconfirmed reports.
The Associated Press quoted an official close to the
discussions as saying that as part of its proposed
final offer the EU had proposed a cut of 70% on
tariffs in the highest of the four 'tiers' under
discussion in the Round.

Bertin eyes move toward North of
Brazil
Brazilian meatpacker Bertin has confirmed that it
hopes to set up a new slaughterhouse in the state of
Acre. But the company has denied that a definitive
decision on a new plant has already been made.
Last week, Acre's state government claimed Bertin
had already decided to invest R$75 million in a new
plant in the city of Rio Branco. State officials said the
facility would have the capacity to slaughter 1,200
bovines per day and be operational in 18 months.
Capacity would be raised to 2,000 head a day by
2010. In the second phase of the project, Bertin will
also produce cooked beef and leather at the new
unit.
But in a statement, Bertin said it was yet to make a
final decision on the project. "Company executives
met with state officials to find out what conditions and
incentives are offered to new companies setting up in
the state," it said.
"We believe in Acre's potential for growth of the next
few years and in the development strategy developed
by its governors. But for the timebeing, we are still
examining the economic viability of the project," it
stated.
By setting up operations in Acre, Bertin would be able
to reach export markets through the Pacific Ocean,
via roads leading to ports in Peru.
The move would also help the company to access
markets with bans on imports from some other
Brazilian states. With its main plant in São Paulo,
Bertin was affected by the European embargo on
cattle after the outbreaks of FMD in Mato Grosso do
Sul and Paraná in 2005. The State of Acre is
considered by the OIE to be free of FMD with
vaccination.
Brazilian leather exports up 34% by
May
Brazilian leather exports grew by 34% in the first five
months of this year compared with the same period in
2006, with an increase in sales from US$703 million
to US$939.70 million, according to the Brazilian
Tanning Industry Centre (CICB).
The president of the CICB, Umberto Cilião Sacchelli,
says "International demand for leather is very strong
and Brazil is the largest producer in the world. With a
strong market, not even an unfavourable exchange
rate can stop sales."
Sacchelli stresses that shipments rose by 8% in
volume terms in the first five months of the year
compared with the same period last year.
The main buyer of Brazilian leather from January to
May in revenue remains Italy (29.12 % share and
growth of 47% on 2006). Next comes China (22.49%
share and growth of 60%) and Hong Kong (10.96%
share and a fall of 7%).
Up to May sales were also up to countries that are
not traditional importers of Brazilian leather, such as
Mexico, the Dominican Republic, and Singapore.

Poultry export revenue up 85% in
May
Brazilian chicken exports reached 273,980 tonnes in
May, 39.4% up on the same month last year,
according to the Brazilian Association of Chicken
Exporters (ABEF). Revenue was US$ 400.8 million,
up 85% in the same period.
Christian Lohbauer, interim president of the body,
says the strong growth in volume and revenue is
down to the weak comparison base from 2006, when
Brazilian exports fell sharply because of AI.
The accumulated export figures for chicken in the first
five months of this year are 1.28 million tonnes, 22.6%
up on the same period in 2006. Revenue is US$ 1.74
billion, up 41.3% in the same comparison period.
The ABEF says the average price for chicken
exported from January to May was US$1,363.00 a
tonne, higher than body's forecast of US$1,200 a
tonne.
Despite the positive figures, the ABEF is maintaining
its modest growth forecasts for 2007, which point to
exports of 2.85 million tonnes and revenue of
US$3.42 billion. These values represent growth of 5%
and 6.8%, respectively, compared with 2006.
Brazilian exporters lose out to
strong local currency
Unfavourable currency movements cost Brazilian
chicken exporters around R$1.14 billion in lost export
earnings from January 2006 to May 2007, according
to a study carried out for the Brazilian Association of
Chicken Producers and Exporters (ABEF).
The Brazilian Real has risen sharply against the US
dollar in the past few months, averaging R$2.14 to
US$1 in the period studied. The study compared
actual export earnings with would have been earned if
the exchange rate had remained stable at R$2.46 to
US$1.
The strength of the Brazilian real has resulted in a fall
in competitiveness for Brazil against its competitors,
such as Thailand (processed chicken) and Argentina
(cuts).
Converting prices to local rates, the values received in
Brazilian reals by Brazilian exporters have increased
less than those received in pesos. The reason is that
whilst the Brazilian real has appreciated 10% against
the US dollar, the peso has risen by 2% to 3%.
Competitiveness in the sector has also fallen because
of price rises for inputs. Considering a base of 100,
whilst corn prices in US dollars rose by about 80
points from January 2006 to April 2007, prices of
chicken cuts rose by 10 points.
Prices rising
The only reason the situation is not worse is because
of the recent rise in chicken prices on the international
market. In May the average price of a tonne was
US$1,463, up 32.6% on the same month in 2006.
Strong demand in Japan, whose stocks are low, and
also in the Middle East and Europe explains higher
prices. "But there is a limit for price rises. No one is
going to pay US$ 1,600 a tonne," says the interim
president of the ABEF, Christian Lohbauer.
The ABEF is to ask the government for the sector to
be included among those that will get benefits to
compensate for losses caused by appreciation of the
Brazilian real. Among the suggested measures is an
immediate cut in tax paid on the acquisition of capital
goods.
Russia suspends pork purchases
from Sadia unit
Russia has suspended imports from a Sadia pork
plant in Três Passos, in Rio Grande do Sul, says Dow
Jones. The suspension enters force on Monday and
has been brought about by the discovery of
salmonella in a 25-tonne load of boned pork from the
unit.
Contacted, Sadia said it had received no official
notification about the suspension. Five poultry plants
have been suspended by Russia recently, for the
same reason.

Domestic Market:
On the domestic market, business is calm. Many

CURRENCY COMMENTS
Fluctuations on the Brazilian currency market remain
intense. News from overseas, especially regarding
possible rises in the basic interest rate in the USA
and other developed countries, led to a momentary
depreciation of the Brazilian real. Soon, however,
news about the Brazilian economy, generally linked
to significant surpluses in the balance of trade, saw
the Brazilian real appreciate again.
From June 15
th
to 21
st
the Brazilian currency market
behaved as it has in recent weeks. After depreciation,
the Brazilian real appreciated again, putting the
currency back at R$ 1.917 to the US dollar on June
21
st
.
Several arguments have been used to explain this
behaviour, varying mainly between fluctuations in US
treasury bonds to the release of data on the US
economy, such as that from the FED in Philadelphia.
Also deemed of importance was the release of a rise
in the reference index for the corporate credit market,
indicating that default protection costs on the old
continent are rising.
Finally, still overseas, the fact that several Wall Street
banks have dismantled hedge fund operations that
had invested heavily in mortgages brought a certain
calm to the market and helped accentuate
oscillations on the currency market.
Nevertheless, most Brazilian currency market
operators are still betting on further appreciation of
the Brazilian real against the US dollar. The Brazilian
balance of trade remains robust, even with the clear
increase in imports, as farm exports are setting
records. The inflow of capital also remains intense
because of the very attractive arbitration opportunity
between the interest rates paid in Brazil and
overseas.
