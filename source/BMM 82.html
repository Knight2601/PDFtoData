Brazilian chick output fails to
keep pace with demand
Brazil will produce about 5
billion broiler chicks this year,
up on last years 4.5 billion,
but still not enough to keep
pace with a surge in demand
as poultrymeat sales expand
both at home and abroad.
In August, Brazilian
production will be around 440
million units, estimates the
Association of Broiler Chick
Producers (APINCO). This is
maximum capacity now, but it
is less than demand, says a
technician at APINCO.
The crisis caused by avian flu
(AI) in Europe and Asia last
year cut the flock of
grandmothers and laying hens
in Brazil, and it takes time for
production to build up again
from a year to a year and a
half. In 2007, the housing of
laying hens will rise by only
5%, says Roberto Kaefer,
president of Globoaves,
Brazils largest broiler chick
producer.
Adding to market pressures,
Brazil exported a significant
volume of fertile eggs at the
beginning of the year, mainly
to Venezuela, where one of
the results of Hugo Chavez
social programs is increased
consumption of chicken.
Countries in Africa and the
Middle East also imported the
product.
We exported 5 million fertile
eggs a month in January and
February. Today, this volume
has fallen to 2.5 million a
month. We have channelled a
greater volume onto the
domestic market, where
demand is also strong and
prices attractive, says
Valdecir Jos Salvador,
commercial manager at
Globoaves.
Importers now looking for
fertile eggs from Brazil are
finding it very difficult to
secure supplies and are
instead buying more chicken.
However, this segment is tight
as well, reflecting the situation
found higher up the
production chain.
As world demand for
poultrymeat rises, prices have
done so, too. The average
price of chicken exported was
US$1,414/tonne in the first
half of the year, up 20.7% on
US$1,170/tonne registered in
the same period last year,
according to the association of
exporters (ABEF).
ABEF estimates that
poultrymeat exports will earn
US$4 billion this year, up on
US$3.5 billion in 2005, before
the crisis that hit exports in
2006, when export sales
totalled US$3.2 billion.

BEEF Market Overview
Last week, business was slow on Brazil's steer
market with prices edging lower.
In So Paulo, the maximum price remained at R$65
per arroba (US$2.20/kg), but few cattle were traded
at this level. Slaughterhouses have been offering
R$62/@ to R$63/@ (US$2.10 to US$2.13/kg), but
most business is being done at R$63/@ to R$64/@
(US$2.10/kg to US$2.17/kg), depending on the size
of lots and how far they are from the
slaughterhouses.
In Mato Grosso do Sul (MS), above all in the
Dourados region, buyers have been pushing hard for
lower prices. Prices offered have fallen from R$62/@
to R$59/@ (US$2.10/kg to US$2.00/kg) - however,
almost no trade has been done at these lower levels.
Most of the lots sold went for R$62/@ and R$61/@
(US$2.10 to US$2.06/kg).
Expectations of a significant rise in steer prices has
lost momentum. Prices should not fall, as the
beginning of the month usually sees increased
demand. But any rises over the next few days will be
small.
On the wholesale beef market, sales remain slow,
and prices have fallen. Demand will increase over the
next few days, and prices may firm up as a result.

ESALQ/BM&F Steer Price Index - So Paulo State
The ESALQ/BM&F Index for live cattle is a weighted average of prices collected in four regions of So Paulo -
Presidente Prudente, Araatuba, Bauru/Marlia and So Jos do Rio Preto. Prices are weighted according to slaughter
volumes in each of these regions. If any reporting slaughterhouse fails to provide data, the relative weight of this unit is
redistributed to other surveyed units.
The Index is calculated by ESALQ (Escola Superior de Agricultura Luiz de Queiroz of University of So Paulo - USP)
and BM&F (Brazilian Board). It was created in 1994 to be used as a reference for the BM&F futures market.

EU brings forward mission to
inspect meat in Brazil
Under pressure from European farmers, the EU has
advised the Brazilian government that is will send a
veterinarian mission to Brazil in October to inspect the
condition of beef exported to the bloc.
The new audit, which had been pencilled in for
November, will be very strict. British and Irish
producers, who have been calling for an embargo on
Brazilian meat, will now demand an investigation into
allegations made in a report submitted by them to the
agriculture commission at the European Parliament.
In March the EU mission claimed there were failings in
Sisbov, the cattle tracking system in Brazil, saying
there were legislative problems with the definition of an
FMD outbreak and failings in "animal identification and
the control of cattle movement."
Now Brussels has said the new mission will get final
proof to determine whether Sisbov has implemented all
the recommendations satisfactorily.
Brazil still paying price for 2005 FMD
outbreak
Almost two years after the FMD outbreak in Mato
Grosso do Sul, the State is still embargoed by over 50
countries. Paran, So Paulo and Santa Catarina also
continue to face restrictions on beef and pork exports.
In addition, six countries have embargoed all meat
exports from Brazil.
So Paulo used to account for about 60% of Brazilian
exports of beef; today it is 20%. "We have lost our two
largest clients: Chile and the EU, and with them over
US$1 billion," says the secretary of agriculture in the
State, Joo Sampaio, in the newspaper Gazeta
Mercantil.

In Paran, the embargos have resulted in losses of
US$570 million (US$285 million), according to
calculations by the local slaughterhouse union.
In Santa Catarina, the Russian embargo remains in
place, and the president of Coopercentral Aurora, in
Chapec, Mrio Lanznaster, estimates that losses to
export have totalled three million head of pigs, worth
over R$600 million (about US$300 million).
Area affected by FMD in 2005 begins
to replace animals
The farms in the municipalities of Mato Grosso do Sul
affected by FMD in 2005 (Eldorado, Japor, and
Mundo Novo) are to restock with cattle, sheep and
goats.
"It is one more step to make use of the region again,
but with complete control," says Ricardo Bacha,
director of IAGRO, the sanitary defence agency in
Mato Grosso do Sul.
Animals can only be put on farms whose herds were
destroyed and had been depopulated. The animals will
be vaccinated against FMD and will be clinically
inspected before getting to the farms, as well as being
tagged.
Eldorado, Japor and Mundo Novo are in the high-
surveillance zone between Brazil and Paraguay.
FMD: Rio Grande do Sul looks for
end to vaccination
Rio Grande do Sul, with the fifth largest cattle herd in
Brazil, may stop vaccinating against FMD as early as
2008.
The State has not had any cases of the disease since
2001 and is now looking for the same sanitary status
obtained by Santa Catarina at the OIE as free of
FMD without vaccination.
OIE discusses FMD control in
Argentina
In order to discuss FMD eradication strategies on the
American continent, the OIE is meeting in Buenos
Aires, Argentina, with representatives from various
countries.
The discussions are part of the Global Framework for
Progressive Control of Transboundary Animal
Diseases set up in April 2005 by the FAO and OIE.
Amongst the diseases being discussed are AI, FMD,
and BSE.
Russians arrive in Brazil
On September 4th veterinarian missions from Russia
arrive in Brazil to inspect the meat production chain.
The inspectors will spend twenty days in the country
and visit various States.
Brazil 'mystified' by UK zebu reports
Brazilian officials have criticised reports made in the
UK press last week, in which two leading pub chains
were accused of serving poor quality beef from
Brazilian zebu cattle.
Following up on a consumer affairs program called
Undercover Mum, several UK newspapers ran stories
saying pub-goers were being fooled into eating inferior
beef from 'humped oxon-like creatures' that 'you may
never have heard of nor seen'.
But officials at the Brazilian Embassy in London have
described the attempt to stigmatise the zebu as
'mystifying'.
"All around the world, the zebu is seen for what it is,
just one among many breeds of cattle. Brazilian cattle,
a significant proportion of which are zebu cross-
breeds, are reared much less intensively than their
counterparts in many other countries," Demetrio
Carvalho, Embassy Commercial Head said in a letter
to the Independent.
As well as a 'whiff of xenophobia', "Brazilians might
also detect the additional whiff of a hidden protectionist
agricultural agenda," he added.
Brazil farm sector to combat
'environmental myths'
Delegates from Brazil's farm sector will visit Europe
next month on a mission to convince customers that
the expanding agricultural business is not harming the
environment.
They intend to show that many of the accusations
made by green activists against Brazilian agriculture --
for example, that increased cane planting is destroying
the Amazon rain forest -- do not reflect reality, Carlo
Lovatelli, president of the Brazilian Agribusiness
Association (Abag), said this week.
"The environmental demand coming from the
international market is very strong and the level of
information from our country on this subject is still low,"
Lovatelli said.
Abag is organising the roadshow together with Brazil's
foreign ministry.

The road show, which will take place in the main
European capitals, will be the first initiative from
Brazil's agriculture sector to counter the claims it is
adding to environmental destruction, Lovatelli said.
Activists groups have long complained that the fast
expansion of Brazil's soy frontier was speeding up the
destruction of forests.
Although producers and the industry have denied most
of the accusations, some of the complaints are
promoting changes in Brazil. About one year ago
Brazilian soy crushers and exporters decided to stop
buying soybeans grown in the Amazon basin.
Consumers, especially in Europe, started to demand
proof of origin and soy industry leaders felt it was time
to take action to prevent buyers from turning to other
sources, reports Reuters.
"We have little problems that have to be addressed
(but) we know a lot of things being said are not true or
at least are being exaggerated," Lovatelli said, citing
soy planting in the Amazon forest.
Brazil concerned over Bolivian FMD
stance
Comments made by the President of Bolivia, Evo
Morales, about combating FMD have concerned the
Brazilian government.
According to reports in the Brazilian press, Bolivia is
not going to prioritise FMD and wants to pass the
problem onto private initiative.
As yet, the Brazilian Agriculture Ministry (MAPA) has
received no official information from Bolivia.
"This is a problem because all the agreements in the
bloc on this issue fall under government responsibility,"
Clio Porto, secretary of international affairs at MAPA
told Gazeta newspaper.
Bolivia's stance could complicate relations between
South Cone countries, which hold a common accord in
combating the disease, and may intensify sanitary
problems in Brazil.
The large cattle-producing Brazilian States of Mato
Grosso and Rondnia border Bolivia. Brazil spends
R$7 million to R$8 million a year on sanitary
surveillance in these States, according to the president
of the Livestock Farming Board, Sebastio Guedes.
"This causes a loss to Brazil, which has to invest in
preventative action," added Guedes. Given the stance
taken by Bolivia, protecting Brazil's sanitary situation
could be about to get tougher.
Steer prices slightly down in Brazil
After more than three months of increasing prices, the
Esalq/BM&F Index for steers, which is the reference
for the Brazilian spot market, has fallen.
According to CEPEA, the centre at the University of
So Paulo that calculates the index, the decrease is
related to strong pressure from slaughterhouses
some cattle farmers would have accepted slightly
lower prices.
After getting close to R$65.00/@ (US$2.20/kg) on
August 15th, the Index has been falling daily, reaching
R$64.27/@ (US$2.15/kg) on August 28th.
Despite the recent fall, CEPEA says the animal supply
is still limited, and the slaughterhouse entry terms in
So Paulo State are around four days.

On August 30th a group of foreign journalists arrives in

The group will include journalists from Italy, Russia,

Marfrig: Bank credit agreement for
cattle suppliers
Banco do Brasil, the main supporting bank for Brazilian
farmers, and the slaughterhouse Marfrig have singed a
protocol that involves credit for costs and investments
to cattle farmers that supply the company.
The vice-president of agribusiness at Banco do Brasil,
Lus Carlos Guedes Pinto, said that R$100 million
(around US$50 million) will be available for the
agreement with Marfrig. Of that total, around R$10
million will be lent directly to Marfrig and R$90 million
to the cattle farmers that supply the company. The
farmers will not necessarily need to be exclusive
suppliers, but they will have to commit part of their
animals to Marfrig.
"Cattle farming in Brazil is moving into a process of
integration," said Guedes. "The agreement with Marfrig
is the first one by Banco do Brasil in this sector and it
could be reproduced for other slaughterhouses", he
added.

Bolivia to allow duty-free beef
imports
Bolivia has suspended all duties on imports of beef
and live cattle in an attempt to prevent hikes in
domestic meat prices.
"The government is to allow duty-free imports of cattle,
calves, cows and breeding animals, along with fresh
and chilled beef," said Rural Development minister
Susana Rivero.
The government said the move was designed to
'prevent speculation and shortages on the domestic
market'.
Uruguay exports more beef to the
USA
From January to July this year, 53% of beef exports
(frozen and chilled) from Uruguay went to the USA
(90,668 tonnes). In that period, average monthly
exports to the US were 12,953 tonnes, compared with
8,774 tonnes in 2006, and 16,767 tonnes in 2005.
Last year, Uruguayan exports were redirected from the
US to markets with better prices, such as Russia,
Israel, Chile, South Africa and the EU, as a result of
Argentina and Brazil's absence from these markets
because of FMD. With the return of these two
countries to these markets, Uruguay has returned to
concentrating on the USA.
Most Uruguayan exports to the USA were frozen
boned beef (96% in 2007). With an annual quota of
just 20,000 tonnes, most of the beef exported to the
USA was outside the quota, paying a tariff of 26.4%.
Russia authorizes Uruguayan
slaughterhouses to export
Russian authorities have confirmed the authorization of
many Uruguayan slaughterhouses to export meat. The
decision was taken after an inspection done by Russia
in Uruguay in April and May this year.
According to the Minister of Agriculture in Uruguay,
Russia has authorized three beef processing plants,
three horsemeat plants and one ovine plant. These
units will be allowed to export directly to Russian
supermarkets. Russia has also approved 14 beef
slaughterhouses to export to Russian processors.
In 2006, Russia was the second largest importer of
Uruguayan meat, after the US.
Australian beef output up in July
Beef production in Australia reached 177,923 tonnes
(carcass weight) in July, up 3.2% on the previous
month, but almost unchanged from July 2006.
The total number of cattle slaughtered (excluding
calves) in July was 646,500 beasts, up 0.9% year on
year and 0.6% month on month, according to data
released by the government's Bureau of Statistics.
Australia exports about two-thirds of its beef
production, with about 90% of shipments going to
Japan, the US and South Korea.
German meat fraud probe widens
Investigations into a meat fraud involving the resale of
slaughter by-products for manufacture into kebabs
have revealed that the unsafe meat has reached a
further eight German states, Dow Jones reports.
The so-called 'K3 material' which is unfit for human or
livestock consumption, was allegedly relabelled by a
company in Bavaria, southeast Germany, and sold on
to a kebab manufacturer in Berlin. It originated from a
trader in Schleswig-Holstein in northern Germany, but
this company does not appear to have acted illegally.
On Thursday last week, the public prosecutor in
Memmingen, Bavaria, and the health authority in Berlin
stated that the Berlin firm had sold its kebabs to take-
away restaurants in the capital and further afield in the
states of Saxony, Saxony-Anhalt, Brandenburg,
Hamburg, Bremen, Schleswig-Holstein, Lower Saxony
and North Rhine-Westphalia.
At one restaurant in Bremen, inspectors found only
one of these frozen kebabs remaining. Berlin health
authority spokeswoman Marie-Luise Dittmar said it
was feared that some of the meat products distributed
by the Berlin firm had already been eaten.
The Berlin authorities say four lots, amounting to 14
tonnes of relabelled meat have so far been traced.
However, the director of the company in Bavaria has
admitted to delivering six lots, amounting to 20 tonnes,
to the Berlin firm in the past few weeks.
It has emerged that the Bavarian company was being
inspected more frequently than normal because its
director had previously served a prison sentence for
subsidy fraud. A neighbour had alerted the authorities
twice to suspicious deliveries at the firm, but
subsequent inspections had revealed nothing. The
regional authorities claim they could not have done any
more to control the company.

Domestic Market Overview
Live Chicken
The market for live chicken remains under the
influence of weak domestic consumption. In So
Paulo State, little business was done at R$1.80/kg
(US$0.90/kg) in the last week, down R$0.05/kg on
the week before.
In the State of Minas Gerais (MG), prices fell more
sharply by R$0.10/kg to R$1.80/kg (US$0.90/kg).
With higher stocks, buyers have been able to take a
step back and put greater pressure on prices. In the
south of Brazil, prices are being sustained by strong
export demand primarily from the Middle East.
Slaughtered Chicken
The market has been slow and large producers are
complaining about the difficulty of passing on price
rises to consumers.
Under pressure, sellers have refused to accept lower
prices, waiting instead for the usual recovery in sales
at the beginning of a new month, when most
Brazilians receive their salaries.
PORK
Domestic Market Overview
The combination of greater supply and weak sales in
So Paulo and Minas Gerais is putting pressure on
pork carcass prices.
The product traded in So Paulo is still facing

POULTRY and PORK
News Summary
Sadia and Perdigo attracted by tax
breaks in Northeast
Tax breaks granted by the Pernambuco government
are attracting giants from the poultry and pork sector.
Sadia and Perdigo have announced plans to build
plants in the State in the northeast of Brazil.
Sadia, the sector leader, is interested in investing
R$60 million in a further processed meat plant in
Pernambuco. Perdigo, the number two in the sector,
has plans to open two plants in the State.
One of the units will process dairy products, and the
other further processed products. In all, the
investments by Perdigo total R$180 million (about
US$90 million).
Genetiporc expands in southeast
Brazil
Canada-based swine genetics company Genetiporc is
to start up DF Pork, a facility designed to meet the
need for high quality breeding animals in southeast
Brazil.
Located in Minas Gerais, the unit is being set up in
partnership with local businessman Jose Geraldo
Ferreira. It will initially have the capacity to produce
3,000 breeding pigs per year.
Ferreira said his decision to invest R$6 milllion in DF
Pork was driven by recent increases in pork prices
and encouraging prospects for exporters.
"We want the facility to be a model of productivity. We
are setting up the whole thing from scratch," he said.
DF Pork will focus on producing breeding animals
from high quality lines, such as Fertilis and G-
Performer Genetiporc already has swine breeding
facilities in Mato Grosso and Rio Grande do Sul.
Pork: Increased demand in China
cheers exporters
The spread of blue ear disease in China has caught
the attention of various countries wanting to get
involved in the probable boost in pork imports by the
country.
Brazil, the fourth largest pork producer in the world,
has stated it is studying the best way to get into the
Chinese market, and will intensify negotiations from
here on.
Meanwhile, countries that are already negotiating with
China have begun to intensify sales, as is the case for
the Danish cooperative, Danish Crowna.
According to a spokesperson from the cooperative,
China is the key to making up for the poor local
situation for pork, which has been facing difficulties
caused by the high price of feed. The company says
demand for pork in China is very strong, allowing price
increases of up to 70% for exports.
Farm inspectors back on strike
from August 28th
Federal farm inspectors in Brazil resumed strike
action on Tuesday, August 28th - once again causing
problems for exporters of meat and other farm goods.
In a memo the association of inspectors (Anffa) said
that the strike would continue until the government
meets its demands.
The association says normal service will remain for
perishable products, and the remainder shall be dealt
with by 30% of the staff.
The inspectors are responsible for loads going
through ports, airports and the country's borders.
Concern over spread of avian flu in
Germany
Nearly 170 000 ducks on a farm in Bavaria, southeast
Germany, have been destroyed after 4 000 of the
flock died of H5N1 avian influenza the biggest cull
since the disease was first found in Germany. It is
thought the outbreak could be linked to a recent
upsurge of cases in wild birds.
This is the second outbreak of the highly pathogenic
form of the disease in Germany this year, the federal
ministry of agriculture and consumer protection stated.
The previous case concerned a goose on a hobby
farm in the neighbouring state of Thuringia on July 6.

After the virus was confirmed by the national
reference laboratory on Saturday (August 25), the
authorities set up a three-kilometre exclusion zone

farm in Wachenroth and the entire flock was

All frozen poultry products that originated from this

The EU has approved the measures taken by the

Russia suspends imports
Russia has reacted to the outbreak by banning
imports of poultry and poultry products from Germany,
the Russian food safety authority Rosselkhoznadzor
said on Wednesday.
Included in the ban are live poultry, uncooked meat,
feed and second hand equipment from poultry farms.
Imports of hatching eggs and day-old chicks from
large German breeding farms are allowed under
guarantee from German veterinary authorities, with
the exception of breeding farms in Bavaria.
Argentine poultrymeat exports rise
again
Exports of fresh and processed poultrymeat from
Argentina totaled $95.7 million from January to July
this year, up 49% on the same period last year --
according to the Food and Animal Standards Agency
(SENASA).
In volume terms, shipments reached 77,820 tonnes, a
29% year-on-year increase.
Of this total, fresh poultrymeat accounted for 48,474
tonnes up 35% on the first seven months of 2006.
Leading destinations for fresh Argentine poultrymeat
in the period were Chile, South Africa, Saudi Arabia
and Germany.
Exports of processed poultrymeat reached 2.437
tonnes in the period a 33% year-on-year rise. The
leading buyers of these products were Germany and
the Netherlands.
Finally, exports of wings and paws, classified by
SENASA as poultry by-products, reached 27,909
tonnes up 25% on Jan-July 2006. Most of this went
to China and Hong Kong.
The number of chickens slaughtered in Argentina in
the first half of this year reached 278 million up 9.3%
on the same period last year. The rise in output
comes in response not just to increased exports but
also to a sharp rise in domestic demand for
poultrymeat. Domestic poultrymeat consumption
reached 646,000 tonnes in the first half of 2007 up
6% on last year.
Spain sets sights on Chinese pork
market
Spanish pigmeat producers should soon be able to
ship their products to China, after the two countries
agreed a protocol on phytosanitary requirements this
week.
Carlos Escribano, livestock director at the Spanish
Agriculture Ministry (MAPA), reached the agreement
with Wang Jun Shuang, head of a mission sent by the
Chinese quarantine service.
After the deal is formally signed possibly in late
September - Spanish pork exports to China will not be
subject to quota limits, Escribano said.
Chinese inspectors recently concluded visits to a
number of Spain's leading pork producers, including
Campofro Alimentacin in Burgos, Palacios
Alimentacin in La Rioja, Embutidos Fermn in
Salamanca and Covap in Crdoba.
Chinese interest will initially focus on buying fresh
pork, rather than higher value cured ham products,
according to Escribano. Efforts are being made to
strike a similar deal giving Spanish poultry exporters
access to the Chinese market, he added.
China's 1.3 billion people consume an average of
more than 37kg of pork each year.
US chick placements increase 2%
Broiler growers in the 19-State weekly program in the
United States placed 178 million chicks for meat
production during the week ending August 25, 2007,
according to the latest USDA AMS market report.
Placements were up 2% from the comparable week a
year earlier. Cumulative placements from December
31, 2006 through to August 25, 2007 were 6.01 billion,
up 1% from the same period a year earlier.
Meanwhile, commercial hatcheries in the 19-State
weekly program set 218 million eggs in incubators

WHO warns on intensive poultry
farming
WHO Director-General Dr Margaret Chan said this
week one of the changes affecting human health was
increasingly intensive poultry farming, which may
account for the global spread of bird flu.
"It should not come as a surprise that we are seeing
more and more disease outbreaks coming from the
animal sector," Chan said.

Much of WHO's annual report on the state of the

to adhere to new, tighter International Health
Regulations, providing the basis for the world to
cooperate in combating diseases.

CURRENCY COMMENTS
The Brazilian currency market has been volatile in the
past week, destabilized by the international markets.
The market has been split between uncertainty over
US interest rates and the greater confidence shown
by investors over profits from the technology sector.
From the beginning of the week to Wednesday, 29
th
,
the US dollar fell from R$2.00/US$1 to almost
R$1.94/US$1.
On Thursday 30
th
, the US dollar rose again with the
beginning of the dispute over formation of the latest
Ptax (average dollar rate) for the month. The rate will
be used for the liquidation of futures contracts due,
which causes a battle between buyers (who are
betting on a strong dollar) and sellers (who are betting
on a fall).
On Friday 31
st
spirits were raised by US President
George W. Bushs announcement of a plan to prevent
greater defaulting on the sub-prime mortgage market.
As the market opened on Friday, the US dollar fell to
R$1.95/US$1.
The Central Bank of Brazil closed the first half of the
year with losses of R$30.3 billion, of which R$29.21
billion was caused by the 9.9% devaluation of the US
dollar against the Brazilian real. The fall of the US
dollar reduced the value of the Central Banks assets
tied to the currency, such as international reserves.
This also resulted in losses in swap deals, in which
the government pays interest to investors and in
exchange gets the variation of the dollar, which was
negative in the period. This loss is 135.43% greater
than the R$12.87 billion in the first half of 2006.
